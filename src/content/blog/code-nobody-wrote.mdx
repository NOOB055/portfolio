A growing share of the diffs arriving in code review are not typed by
the person who opens them. Depending on where you work, that sentence
lands somewhere between "obviously" and "not in *this* codebase" — and
in regulated environments the reflex is often prohibition.

I think the prohibition reflex misreads what CI/CD was for in the first
place.

A deployment pipeline is an admission-control system for untrusted
changes. That is its entire job. And the uncomfortable truth is that the
changes were never trustworthy *before* the machines showed up — humans
write plausible-looking bugs at two in the morning, copy code from old
Stack Overflow answers, and introduce vulnerable dependencies with
complete confidence. The gate was never *just* "do we trust the
author." It was "did the change survive the checks." If your controls
only worked because authors were careful, they were never controls.

## What stays the same

Most of the playbook for AI-authored changes is the playbook you should
already have:

- **Tests are the spec.** A generated change with green, meaningful
  tests comes with evidence; a generated change without them is a guess
  with good posture. The discipline of demanding behavioral proof predates
  the tools and survives them.
- **Scanning does not care about authorship.** Static analysis, secret
  detection, and dependency scanning ask "what does this code do," not
  "who wrote it." They are exactly as necessary as they were before.
- **Small diffs or no review.** Review capacity becomes the bottleneck
  when generation is cheap. The countermeasure is structural: keep
  changes small enough that a human can actually hold them, because a
  thousand-line PR gets approved on vibes regardless of its author.

## What actually changes

Two things do change.

**Dependency suggestion is a new supply-chain surface.** Generative
tools sometimes propose packages that are obscure, abandoned, or — in
the worst case — don't exist until someone registers the name and fills
it with something hostile. Lockfiles, registry allowlists, and "a new
dependency is an architectural decision, not an import statement" stop
being hygiene advice and start being load-bearing.

**Accountability cannot be generated.** Change control in the
environments I work in — PCI DSS, SOC 2 — ultimately attaches every
production change to an accountable human. A tool can author a change; it cannot own one. The
approver who merges is asserting "I understand this and accept
responsibility" — and that assertion is the one part of the process
that must not be automated away. "The model wrote it" will never be an
acceptable answer in an incident review.

The teams that handle this well won't be the ones with the strictest
bans or the loudest enthusiasm. They'll be the ones whose pipelines
were honest about untrusted input all along — for whom the new authors
are just more traffic through a gate that was already built.
