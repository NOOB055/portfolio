Compliance audits of a deployment process keep circling the same four
questions. What changed? Who approved it? When did it reach production?
And if it had gone wrong, could you have undone it?

The traditional way to answer those questions is archaeology. Someone
spends a sprint pulling ticket exports, chasing approval emails, and
screenshotting consoles, trying to reconstruct a paper trail for changes
that happened months ago. The evidence is sampled, lossy, and assembled
under deadline pressure — which is a strange way to prove that a process
is under control.

The GitOps answer is structurally different: the system that *performs*
the change is the same system that *records* it. There is no second
artifact to assemble, because the deployment mechanism is the paper
trail.

## Mapping the controls

Take the standard change-management expectations and put them next to a
reasonably configured GitOps setup — ArgoCD, in the environments I work
in:

- **Authorization.** A protected branch with required review means an
  unapproved change does not have a sanctioned path to production. The
  control is not a policy document asking people to behave; it is a
  permission model in which the bad action does not exist.
- **Attribution and timestamps.** Every change is a commit and a merge
  with a reviewer, timestamped and tamper-evident — immutable in
  practice once force-pushes are blocked, with attribution riding on
  platform identity rather than the self-asserted author line. Nobody
  writes this down because nobody has to.
- **Segregation of duties.** Author and approver are distinct
  identities, enforced by the platform rather than by trust.
- **Rollback.** A revert is a first-class operation, and your history
  of reverts is *evidence that rollback works in practice* — something
  a disaster-recovery binder can only claim.
- **Drift detection.** A reconciliation loop that converges the runtime
  toward the declared state is a continuous control. The interesting
  audit question inverts: it is no longer "how do you know production
  matches the approved configuration?" but "show me an alert from the
  last time it didn't."

## What it doesn't buy you

Honesty matters here, because "we do GitOps" is not a compliance
strategy by itself.

Git history proves nothing if the repository's controls are weak — a
repo where anyone can push to main, or where review is a rubber stamp
from a teammate's second account, produces beautiful-looking evidence of
an uncontrolled process. Break-glass access paths, changes to
infrastructure that lives outside the repository, secrets handling, and
access reviews all still need their own answers. And auditors are
entitled to test the control, not just admire the tooling: expect to
demonstrate that an unreviewed change actually gets rejected.

The goal is narrower and more useful: every question about *changes*
should be answerable from systems of record that were going to exist
anyway. Audit preparation time for the deployment process trends toward
zero, not because anyone worked harder, but because evidence became a
byproduct of shipping.

Screenshots are what you produce when the system of record is somewhere
else. Put the system of record in the deploy path, and the audit writes
itself as you work.
